# 3. The Future Self Principle

## Put the future self first, not the self that only wants relief.

The future self is the version of you that has to live with the choices made by your current self. 

While this sounds simple, it changes everything. Most people make decisions under the pressure of the moment, reacting with feelings of anger, fear, pride, shame, desire, panic, or exhaustion. They choose to say something, avoid an issue, hide their feelings, buy something, eat something, send a message, remain silent, walk away, explode, submit, control, or collapse; often without considering the long-term effects. Later, when the moment has passed, another version of themselves has to live with the consequences of those choices.

This later version is the one most people forget about. The future self wakes up to face the consequences. The future self must deal with the distance created by an argument. The future self has to rebuild trust after telling a lie. They have to bear the resentment that comes from people-pleasing. The future self must explain why the same patterns keep repeating. They have to examine their relationships, their body, their bank account, their home, their children, their habits, their silence, the damage done, the missed opportunities, and ask, “What now?”

That is why proper self-interest must take time into account.

If you focus solely on your current self, you become short-sighted. You cater to the part of you that seeks immediate relief while ignoring the part that craves lasting peace. You prioritize winning over connection and avoid discomfort instead of embracing a life built on truth.

The principle of considering your future self is straightforward:
Act in the best interest of the version of yourself who will face the consequences of your actions.

This doesn’t mean you should always choose the hard path simply because it is hard. It doesn't mean you must be rigid, joyless, or overly responsible to the point of draining your energy. Your future self isn't a prison guard; instead, it represents the part of you seeking care that endures beyond your current mood.

Sometimes, your future self needs rest. At other times, it seeks joy, a clear "no," courage, softness, or even the wisdom to stop explaining and simply go to bed. Your future self may require you to apologize when it's warranted or to stop apologizing for things that aren't your fault. It isn't always about discipline; it's about alignment with your deeper needs.

The key question to ask yourself is:
Will this choice help me become someone I can trust?

This question cuts through a lot of distractions. It is not about:
- “Will this make me look good?”
- “Will this get them off my back?”
- “Will this allow me to win?”
- “Will this stop my discomfort immediately?”

Instead, focus on: 
“Will this help me trust myself?” 

Self-trust is not built by simply thinking positive thoughts about yourself. It is developed when you make choices that align with your values, even when it would be easier to do otherwise. You start to trust yourself when you tell the truth, despite the temptation to hide. You build trust when you set boundaries before resentment takes hold. You reinforce your self-trust when you repair relationships without making excuses. You strengthen this trust when you can experience strong emotions without letting them control your actions.

This level of self-trust is crucial in relationships.

If you have broken someone's trust, your first instinct may be to focus on their mistrust. You might want them to stop questioning you, stop hurting, stop bringing it up, stop doubting you, or stop looking at you with that disapproving face. You may wish for the repair process to move faster because their pain constantly reminds you of what happened. However, the deeper question to consider is not just, “When will they trust me again?” but rather, “Can I trust myself now?”

Can you trust yourself to tell the truth even when it costs you? Can you trust yourself to bear their pain without defensiveness? Can you trust yourself to stay honest even when they are not ready to believe you? Can you trust yourself to become someone whose behavior no longer requires management?

Their trust may come later, but yours needs to begin now. This is not meant to dismiss their pain, as it matters greatly. Their mistrust may be entirely understandable. However, if your entire focus is on getting them to trust you again, you may still be trying to manage their experience. The work involves becoming trustworthy. Strive to be someone who doesn’t need to be chased, checked, monitored, or decoded; someone whose actions speak louder than words of reassurance.

This is the future self principle in action. It shifts the responsibility back where it belongs: not in controlling the other person, but in controlling your own actions.

This principle can be applied in various situations beyond betrayal. In conflict, it prompts you to ask, “Will I be glad I said this in this way?” In parenting, it encourages you to consider, “What is this teaching?” In the workplace, it makes you reflect, “What pattern am I building?” In terms of health, it asks, “Am I caring for the body I still need to live in?” In friendships, it urges you to think, “Am I being clear, or am I hoping they can guess?” In your private life, it raises the question, “Would I respect this choice if no one ever knew?”

The last point is important because character is developed in private long before it becomes evident in public. Your future self is always observing; not in a creepy way, but as a simple matter of reality. A part of you is aware of the choices you make. This part records your compromises, courage, avoidance, honesty, cheap shots, and moments of genuine restraint. You cannot completely escape your own inner witness.

This is why the idea of “getting away with it” is often a myth. You might evade social consequences, avoid being found out, or dodge immediate repercussions. However, you cannot escape becoming the person who made those choices. You don’t get away from conditioning yourself to take that path. Ultimately, you cannot avoid the quiet erosion of self-trust.

The principle of the future self is not about inducing shame. Shame tells you, “You are bad.” This principle, however, encourages you to “pay attention to what this is building.” It doesn’t need to crush you; it is meant to awaken you.

When you begin to consider your future self in your decisions, your choices become clearer; not perfect, but clearer. You start to pause more often, communicate with greater care, and recognize when you are about to trade long-term peace for short-term relief. You become aware of moments when pride might cost you a connection or when avoidance masquerades as wisdom. You also notice when people-pleasing creates a debt that the other person never agreed to pay.

The future self provides you with distance from your impulses and allows you to ask, “Once this feeling passes, what will I wish I had done?” This question does not eliminate emotion, but it prevents emotion from becoming your sole guide.

This approach represents adult self-interest. It doesn’t involve denying the current self; your feelings, needs, and limits are important too. However, the current self isn’t the only one that matters. The future self deserves a voice at the table as well.

A life focused solely on the present self can become chaotic. Conversely, a life centered exclusively on the future self may turn dry and punishing. The goal is not to neglect the present; rather, it is to allow the present and future to communicate with one another. You care for the now without compromising the later moments, and you care for the future without dismissing the present.

Finding that balance is where wisdom begins.

At times, your future self may ask you to endure discomfort because honesty is essential. Other times, it may urge you to stop enduring, as you might be confusing loyalty with self-abandonment. It might call for repair in relationships or even suggest that you leave. Sometimes, it will encourage you to speak up, while at other times, it will advise you to hold your tongue until you can express yourself clearly.

The answers are not always the same; that is why this is not a rulebook but rather a principle. A rulebook dictates, "Always do this," while a principle encourages you to "Look closely."

Examine the consequences. Recognize the patterns. Consider what this choice does to your ability to trust yourself. Reflect on whether this decision brings you closer to the person you aspire to become.

The future self principle provides moral weight without being preachy. It makes doing good practical. Speak the truth because you have to live with it. Set boundaries because resentment is costly. Make repairs because distance amplifies issues. Collaborate because life tends to repeat itself. Say no when necessary, as insincere affirmatives poison love. Choose wisely, as every decision shapes the next version of you.

You are not only addressing the present moment; you are also sculpting the person who will face the next moment.

That is why prioritizing yourself correctly always points toward your future self. The current self may be loud, the wounded self may be urgent, and the defensive self may be persuasive. However, it is the future self who inherits the world they are building.

Before you act, ask yourself this crucial question:  
**What choice helps me become someone I can trust?**

This question won't make life easier, but it will make it clearer. Clarity is often the most genuine kindness you can offer to your future self.
