# 1. Put Yourself First, Properly

## The problem is not self-interest. The problem is bad self-interest.

You should put yourself first because because your life is the only one you are directly responsible for steering. This doesn’t mean that other people don’t matter; rather, it highlights that you cannot exclude yourself from the equation and expect a healthy outcome. You are always involved in your choices, even when you try to act as if you’re not. You are present in the "yes" you give when you actually mean "no." You are present in the silence you use to avoid discomfort. You are present in the anger you justify because you feel hurt. You are present in the truth you avoid, the apology you postpone, the boundary you refuse to establish, and the resentment you allow to grow in silence.

Many people believe that putting themselves first means choosing their own comfort, preferences, or immediate desires. This is a superficial understanding. While comfort and preference do matter, they are not sufficient for a fulfilling life. If you let your desire for relief dictate every decision, you will eventually create a life that requires constant relief from itself. You will avoid difficult conversations, resist necessary changes, maintain a fragile peace, say things that keep you safe in the moment, and then wonder why your relationships feel tense, distant, or dishonest.

Proper self-interest is not about doing whatever feels good in the moment. It involves asking what is truly beneficial for you when considering the long-term consequences of your choices. This shift in perspective can change everything. Instead of asking, “What do I want to do?” you start asking, “What does this choice create?” Instead of focusing on “How do I win?” you consider, “What kind of person do I become if I continue making this choice?” Instead of inquiring, “How do I stop feeling uncomfortable?” you ask, “What truth is this discomfort prompting me to face?”

This is why poor self-interest can be so destructive. It protects immediate feelings but often undermines the future. For example, someone may lie to avoid conflict, but then must live with the instability of being someone who hides the truth. Another person may shout to feel powerful, yet must deal with a relationship where their partner feels unsafe. Someone who pleases others to gain approval may end up feeling resentment, exhaustion, and the quiet realization that no one truly knows them. Additionally, a person who tries to control their partner to alleviate anxiety may end up with a partner who feels managed rather than understood.

None of these behaviors represent real self-interest. They offer short-term solutions at long-term costs, which is fundamentally flawed. These approaches may provide temporary relief but can make future situations more difficult. This kind of selfishness is justifiably distrusted. It’s not that self-interest is inherently wrong, but rather that immature self-interest fails to recognize the consequences of its actions.

Proper self-interest takes a long-term perspective. It considers the future self, the relational climate, the trust being built, the emotional habits being formed, and the character being developed. It recognizes that every repeated action becomes a pattern. A single sharp comment might be overlooked, but a pattern of contempt alters the atmosphere in the room. One instance of avoiding the truth might be excusable, but a consistent pattern of avoidance teaches others not to expect honesty from you. A single defensive reaction can be a normal human response, but a repeated pattern of defensiveness makes close relationships challenging.

This is why goodness and self-interest are not adversaries. When you focus solely on the short term, goodness may feel like a burden; it might seem like honesty, patience, repair, boundaries, and accountability are sacrifices you make for others at your own expense. However, when you understand the consequences of your actions, these qualities become clearly self-serving in the most profound way. Honesty provides you with stability. Accountability strengthens you. Boundaries safeguard genuine love from resentment. Repair prevents connections from deteriorating. Patience allows you to avoid reacting impulsively to fleeting emotions as if they were wisdom.

This does not mean you should become endlessly accommodating. It does not mean you should tolerate poor treatment or diminish yourself for the sake of connection. That is not practical morality; it is a different form of confusion. Proper self-interest does not erase your identity; it embraces it fully. It considers what serves your well-being, integrity, future, and your ability to maintain honest relationships with reality. 

Sometimes, this means softening your stance. Sometimes, it means apologizing. Other times, it may mean saying no, staying calm, leaving the room, or even leaving the relationship. The approach may vary, but the principle remains the same: choose the action that serves the highest good you can genuinely support.

That is why "put yourself first" needs to be accompanied by the word "properly." Without it, the phrase can lead to ego-driven behaviour. With it, it becomes a pathway to maturity. Putting yourself first properly means not handing the steering wheel to the loudest parts of yourself. Your anger may hold important information, but it shouldn’t dictate your entire narrative. Your fear might identify real dangers, but it shouldn’t cause you to contract your world. Your pride might seek protection, but it shouldn’t come at the expense of truth. Your desire for peace can be valid, but it shouldn’t lead to avoidance.

The adult self must take the lead. Not the perfect self, not the saintly self, and not the self that never reacts, never struggles, and never makes mistakes. The adult self is simply the part of you that can pause long enough to consider the consequences of your actions. It is the part that can ask, “Will this decision bring me closer to the life I want to create?” It recognizes that while a reaction may feel satisfying, it could come at a high cost. It understands that a clear “no” is better than a resentful “yes,” that a difficult truth is superior to a managed lie, that an attempt at repair is preferable to silent pride, and that connection achieved through control is not true connection at all.

When you prioritize yourself properly, you stop letting others dictate your character. You do not become cruel because someone else is difficult. You do not become dishonest because being honest is uncomfortable. You do not try to control situations because you feel unsafe. You do not become passive simply because conflict is challenging. You do not grow resentful because you failed to communicate clearly. Instead, you begin to take responsibility for your own actions and choices in the world.

This responsibility is not a burden in a negative sense; it is freedom. It provides you with something solid to stand on. You may not be able to control how others respond, nor can you force trust, repair, love, understanding, maturity, or honesty from them. However, you can become someone whose actions are more thoughtful. You can be someone who tells the truth sooner. You can set boundaries without resentment. You can give without keeping score. You can maintain your dignity without needing to win every argument.

This is what true selfishness looks like. Not selfishness as a form of extraction, but as stewardship. You are taking care of your future, your character, your peace, your relationships, and your ability to trust yourself. You are making choices that help you live authentically and without needing to escape your own life.

So yes, prioritize yourself. But be mindful of which self you are prioritizing. Put your future self first. Put your adult self first. Put your honest self first. Put the self that can stand firmly while still caring. Put the self that seeks connection without control, boundaries without cruelty, peace without avoidance, and love without sacrificing your own needs.

This is not shallow selfishness; it is selfishness done properly. And once you embrace selfishness in this meaningful way, it begins to resemble wisdom.
