# 5. Do Not Turn People Into Props

## The moment another person becomes a tool, you stop meeting them as a person.

One of the quickest ways to damage a relationship is to stop seeing the other person as a person. While this might seem obvious, it can happen quietly and subtly. At times, this behavior may not appear cruel from the outside; it might even look like concern, advice, or "I’m just trying to help." It can take the form of planning, managing, fixing, improving, guiding, protecting, or maintaining stability.

However, beneath the surface, something has changed. The other person is no longer being genuinely met; instead, they are being handled. They turn into a problem to solve, a mood to manage, a reaction to prevent, a risk to control, a source of reassurance, a threat to neutralize, a role to maintain, or a prop in the narrative you are trying to uphold.

When someone becomes a prop, you lose the ability to see them clearly. You no longer connect with the person in front of you; instead, you see them through the lens of how they serve your needs. You perceive them based on the comfort they provide, the fears they evoke, the approval they offer, the shame they induce, the stability they bring, or the risks they represent. 

This is where control distorts perception. Once you start trying to manage someone to ensure their behavior is safe for you, you begin losing touch with who they truly are. You may still love and care for them deeply; you might even be afraid of losing them. But fear can quickly turn care into control. Instead of asking, “Who are you?” you start asking, “How do I get you to be the version of yourself that calms me down?” That is a fundamentally different question.

This dynamic occurs frequently in relationships. One person may become the reassurance supplier, while another becomes the emotional stabilizer. One might turn into proof of worth, while another assumes the role of the villain. There can be parents and children, projects and threats, audiences and mirrors. In such cases, the individual becomes lost within their assigned function.

That is dangerous because the relationship may still seem active. People may continue to talk, organize, plan, argue, explain, apologize, and try. But the engagement is missing. The other person is no longer being seen as a full human being with their own inner world; instead, they are being molded to make your internal experience feel more manageable.

This is why micromanagement creates distance, even when it claims to foster closeness. If you are constantly adjusting someone, correcting them, prompting them, checking in on them, monitoring them, steering them, interpreting their behavior, or shaping their actions so that you can feel okay, they may become more predictable. However, they won't feel more understood. They may become more compliant, but they will not feel more connected. They may learn the rules, but they will also learn that your comfort matters more than their aliveness.

That is not a partnership; it is stage management. No one wants to be loved as a managed object.

This is where practical morality becomes very clear. If you use another person merely as a means to your own end, something breaks—not only in them but in you as well. You damage your capacity to perceive them fully. You train yourself to see people only through the lens of usefulness, threat, comfort, or control. You stop asking what is true and start asking what works. 

That shift is subtle, but it is significant. “What works” can become a dangerous question when it is disconnected from respect. Manipulation works sometimes. Pressure sometimes works. Silence can work. Charm, guilt, fear, and withholding can also work. If your only question is, “Did I get the response I wanted?” you may mistake effectiveness for goodness.

However, a person is not a vending machine for emotional outcomes. You cannot reduce them to the response you want and still call it love.

This does not mean that influence is wrong. Relationships are full of influence. You affect others, and they affect you. Tone matters. Timing matters. Words matter. Warmth matters. Repair matters. The goal is not to pretend you have no impact; rather, the goal is to stop using that impact as a form of covert control.

There is a clear difference between invitation and manipulation. Invitation respects the other person's freedom, while manipulation tries to bypass it. 

Invitation says, “Here is where I am. Here is what I feel. Here is what I want. Here is what I am asking.” Manipulation, on the other hand, says, “How do I get you to move where I need you without fully showing myself?” 

That is the difference: one approach meets the other person, while the other manages them.

This is why honesty matters so much. Honest communication allows both people to remain authentic. It gives the other person the dignity of knowing where they stand. It does not guarantee agreement, but it preserves contact. It allows the other person to respond to the truth rather than to a performance designed to produce a specific outcome.

Control does the opposite. Control hides the real aim; it tries to shape the other person's behavior without fully revealing the underlying needs. Instead of saying, “I feel scared and I need reassurance,” it interrogates. Instead of saying, “I feel unimportant and I want more effort,” it criticizes. Instead of saying, “I am hurt and I need repair,” it punishes. Instead of saying, “I feel insecure,” it monitors.

The hidden need may be understandable, but the approach costs too much. The cost is contact.

When you start viewing someone as something to manage, you lose the opportunity to truly connect with them. You miss out on discovering whether they would have shown care, responded genuinely, repaired your relationship, understood you, or reached out freely. What you may observe are behaviors, but they do not provide real evidence of love. That’s a lonely trade-off.

To experience love freely, you must allow the other person to be free. This can be a daunting realization. You cannot force someone to say a genuine yes. You cannot control them into experiencing real desire. You cannot manipulate them into being truly honest. You cannot pressure them into showing real care. While you might achieve compliance or hear reassuring words, the part of you that knows the difference will still recognize the lack of authenticity.

This is why the urge for control can become addictive. It offers just enough relief to keep you going, but it doesn’t provide lasting peace. You check in and feel better temporarily. You ask questions and feel a momentary sense of relief. You manage the situation, and you feel better for a while. You correct behaviors, and again, there’s a brief comfort. But the relief dissipates because it is not rooted in trust—it is based on management.

Then, you find yourself needing another fix: more checking, more explanations, more rules, more pressure, more emotional monitoring, and more attempts to prevent the outcomes you fear. With each attempt, the other person becomes less of an individual and more of an object to manage.

This dynamic doesn’t only emerge from anxious control; it can also occur through emotional withdrawal. You might turn someone into a mere prop by casting them as the villain in your narrative. You stop seeing their pain because you need them to be wrong. You stop acknowledging their humanity because your defense relies on portraying them as unreasonable, needy, dramatic, cold, selfish, or impossible. 

Once you label them in this way, everything they do gets filtered through that lens. If they seek closeness, they are deemed needy. If they ask for space, they are seen as cold. If they cry, they are manipulated. If they remain calm, it’s interpreted as indifference. If they challenge you, they are seen as attacking. If they go quiet, they are accused of punishment.

At that point, you are no longer engaging with them as an individual; you are interacting with the character you have assigned to them. This illustrates why perception is such a moral issue. How you perceive someone influences how you treat them. If you see them as an enemy, you may justify using weapons against them. If you view them as a child, you might think control is warranted. If you regard them as a project, you’ll feel justified in correcting them. If you see them as a prop, you’ll rationalize managing them. But if you recognize them as a whole person, you must return to respect.

Respect does not equate to agreement. Respect does not mean tolerating harm. Respect does not require you to stay close to everyone. Respect involves not reducing someone to a function before deciding how to interact with them. 

You can say no to someone while still seeing them clearly. You can leave a relationship without demonizing the other person. You can set boundaries without diminishing their dignity. You can recognize harm without erasing their humanity. That’s true power.

True power is far cleaner than control. You reach for control when you distrust the situation. Clean power emerges when you reclaim your own agency. You stop asking, “How do I make you become who I need you to be?” and start asking, “How do I respond honestly to who you are showing me you are?” 

That question brings you back to reality.

Reality can be painful, but it grounds you. If someone is not choosing you, it’s essential to acknowledge that. If someone is unsafe, you need to recognize it. If someone is willing but clumsy, you should be aware of that too. If someone loves you but cannot read your mind, it’s important to understand that. If someone is trying, but not in the exact way you imagined, you should know that as well.

You won’t be able to grasp any of this if you’re too busy managing the evidence. Allow people to show you who they really are.

This doesn’t mean you should passively wait; it means you should actively engage. Be honest. Make requests. Set boundaries. Offer repairs. Ask questions. Then, observe what happens without trying to manipulate the outcome.

This process can be difficult because it often requires you to grieve. You might have to mourn the idea that the other person isn’t the fantasy version you envisioned. You may need to grieve the fact that they cannot provide what you want. It’s possible you’ll have to accept that they can give it, but only freely, not under pressure. You might also need to come to terms with the limitations of the relationship. On the flip side, you may discover that they were more available than your fears allowed you to see.

Regardless, you will move closer to the truth. And the truth is ultimately better for you than a managed illusion.

That's why the idea of “not turning people into props” is essential to practical morality. It’s not just about being kind to others; it’s also in your best interest to see people clearly. When you’re rooted in reality, you make better choices, waste less energy on managing performances, and build trust based on evidence rather than control. You stop confusing compliance with love and predictability with connection.

A real relationship requires two complete individuals:  
Not one person and one prop.  
Not one person and one project.  
Not one person and one emotional support machine.  
Not one person and one villain.  

It takes two people—messy, limited, evolving, choosing, failing, repairing, revealing, and meeting each other where they can. 

That is riskier than control, but it’s also the only place where genuine connection can flourish.
