## Why Being Selfish Is the Only Option

You should prioritise yourself. It's important to say this clearly because many people often interpret it as selfish, cold, arrogant, or morally questionable. It might sound like justifying poor behaviour or treating immaturity as self-care, but that’s not the intention here. In fact, the message is quite the opposite.

Much of what is labelled as selfishness is not true self-interest; it often stems from impulse, defensiveness, fear, or ego. It's a way for someone to seek temporary relief while unintentionally harming their future self.

Putting yourself first in a meaningful way means making choices that are in the best interests of the person you will become and who will have to bear the consequences of those decisions. That future self is important. The version of you that is calm, mature, and responsible matters. You will have to live with the trust you’ve built or broken, the body you’ve cared for or neglected, the relationships you’ve strengthened or damaged, and the patterns you’ve either disrupted or repeated.

Instead of simply asking, “What do I want?” ask yourself, “What will this create?”

At the core of practical morality lies a profound understanding of goodness. It is not merely a sentimental idea; it does not mean being nice, overly accommodating, or sacrificing your own well-being for others' comfort. When properly understood, goodness embodies intelligent self-interest while considering the consequences of our actions. It reflects the realisation that life, relationships, families, trust, and character are not defined by isolated actions; instead, they are ongoing processes. Each interaction builds upon the last, teaching those around you the nature of your relationship with them.

This is why honesty is essential. It’s not just a moral absolute; lying generates instability. Boundaries are important not because they are trendy concepts, but because without them, care can turn into resentment. Cooperation matters not because you must surrender, but because the person in front of you is not your automatic enemy. Repair is crucial; it’s not about grovelling but recognising that unaddressed conflicts create distance. Accountability is significant not because someone needs to blame, but because shunning responsibility weakens you.

The argument here is straightforward, yet it has profound implications: choosing to do good is the only intelligent option because any other choice harms the system in which you live. However, this does not imply that you should trust everyone, remain in harmful situations, allow others to use or control you, or let them shame or drain you. True self-interest involves discernment; it encompasses setting clear boundaries, saying no when necessary, maintaining distance when needed, and walking away without resorting to cruelty. It also means expressing the truth without weaponising it.

This is not a call for selflessness. Misunderstood selflessness often leads to people-pleasing under a different label. It manifests as giving while secretly keeping a tally, saying yes while harbouring resentment, and pretending to have no needs before blaming others for not fulfilling them. That behaviour does not embody love; it is not virtuous; it is merely confusion.

The goal is clearer than that. You prioritise yourself by becoming a person you can trust; someone who can express anger without being destructive, feel fear without being driven by it, desire connection without attempting to control others, give freely, say no firmly, repair relationships honestly, and stand strong without the need to dominate.

Practical morality begins where mature self-interest evolves. It encourages you to stop confusing relief with peace, winning with success, control with safety, sacrifice with love, and comfort with freedom. It asks you to be honest enough to recognise what your choices are truly creating. Because reality keeps track of your actions, and sooner or later, you will live in a world shaped by your repeated decisions.
