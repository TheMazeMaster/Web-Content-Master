# 8. The Therapy Room Version

## Do not ask how to control them. Ask who you need to become so your next move is clean.

In relationships, questions often arise in disguise. We may ask ourselves:

“How do I get them to understand?”

“How do I make them stop doing that?”

“How do I get them to open up?”

“How do I get them to care?”

“How do I get them to trust me again?”

“How do I get them to change?”

These questions seem reasonable, especially when someone feels hurt, tired, frightened, or out of patience. It makes sense to want the other person to change. If they softened, things might feel easier. If they listened, it could feel safer. If they changed, there might be a chance for improvement.

However, there is a problem hidden within these questions. The underlying assumption is about control. It implies that your well-being depends on the other person’s actions. It suggests, “I need them to change before I can feel okay.” While there are moments when the other person does need to transform, and their behaviour might actually be damaging, the fundamental approach shouldn’t start with, “How do I change them?”

A more constructive question is: “Who do I need to become so that my next move is honest, mature, and aligned with the outcome I desire?” This question shifts the focus back to you.

It does not hold you responsible for the other person's behaviour. It doesn’t imply that you can fix the relationship alone. It doesn’t position you as the sole mature party while the other person does whatever they want. That wouldn’t be empowerment; it would be self-abandonment.

The key point is simpler: your work begins with your actions, your tone, your honesty, your boundaries, your courage, your willingness to repair, your timing, your readiness to listen, and your willingness to stop performing. It involves expressing your feelings directly rather than letting them leak out. It means recognising the patterns you help create, not just the patterns you endure.

This is where practical morality becomes actionable. A person might come in saying, “They never listen to me.” While that may be true, the focus cannot remain solely on accusation. The pivotal question becomes, “How do you try to be heard?” Do you ask clearly, or do you attack? Do you express your needs, or do you lead with criticism? Do you invite contact or test them, waiting for failure? Do you speak from pain or from a place of blame?

Another person may say, “They do not care.” This could also be accurate. Yet again, the exploration cannot stop there. How do you recognise care? Would you be able to accept it if it appeared differently than you expected? Are you seeking love or demanding proof? Do you want connection, or do you pursue control disguised as connection?

Someone else might say, “I cannot trust them.” While this mistrust may be justified and your instincts might be accurate, even then, the question should be, “What do you need in order to stay honest with yourself?” It’s not about forcing yourself to trust or punishing them until you feel safe. Instead, ask yourself, “What would give you real information? What boundaries will protect you? What behaviours need to be consistent? What choices will keep you aligned as you wait to see what is true?”

That is a different kind of conversation. It moves away from managing the other person and towards gaining clarity within yourself. This isn’t easy because blame is seductive. Blame provides the mind with somewhere to land; it says, “There. That is the problem.” Sometimes it points at something real; the other person may have lied, withdrawn, attacked, dismissed, cheated, neglected, controlled, or avoided. Naming those behaviours is important. Pretending that harm did not occur is not maturity.

However, blame alone does not create movement. It can identify a wound, but it cannot heal the relationship. It can describe a behaviour, but it cannot dictate your next constructive move. It can reveal what hurt you, but it cannot become the entire foundation of your life. When blame becomes your main operating system, you lose agency. You become trapped in the belief that your life can only improve when the other person finally becomes who you need them to be. While this may feel powerful, it is not; it is simply dependency with a louder voice.

Agency asks for something braver. It prompts questions like, “Given what is true, how do I now move?” If the other person is willing, how do you engage that willingness without dragging the entire past into each interaction? If they are unwilling, how do you stop bargaining with reality? If they are clumsy but trying, how do you provide clear feedback without turning every mistake into proof? If they are unsafe, how do you protect yourself without needing hatred to justify it?

That is the adult position; neither passive nor controlling, not naive nor cruel, but clear.

In relationship work, people often seek a technique that will elicit the desired response from the other person, a sentence, a script, a strategy, or the perfect wording that finally unlocks them. There’s nothing wrong with wanting better language; communication matters. Tone matters. Timing matters. However, if the deeper aim is control, even the best sentence becomes a tool of pressure. You can articulate the right words from the wrong place.

For example, you can say, “I feel hurt,” while trying to induce guilt in someone. You can say, “I need space,” while attempting to punish. You can say, “I understand,” while waiting for your chance to win. You can say, “I love you,” while trying to stop someone from leaving. The words may be clean, but the intention behind them is not.

This is why the work needs to go deeper than mere communication tips. The real question is not just, “What should I say?” It is, “Where am I speaking from?”

Am I speaking from a place of adult clarity or from a position of wounded defensiveness? Am I trying to invite connection or secure a specific outcome? Am I genuinely willing to listen to them, or only willing to be heard? Am I speaking the truth or just managing their reaction? Am I asking for repair, or demanding emotional repayment?

That level of honesty truly changes the room.

When you become clearer within yourself, the quality of your relationships improves. The other person may still not respond positively, but at least your communication is clearer. They’re no longer reacting to the fog of your resentment, tests, hints, control, or performance; they are responding to something more genuine.

This is important because many relationships don’t end due to one significant event but rather slowly deteriorate through unclear communication. For instance:

A need may manifest as criticism.

A fear may come across as control.

A hurt may be expressed as sarcasm.

A boundary may be interpreted as withdrawal.

A wish may be voiced as a complaint.

A longing may be felt as pressure.

Both individuals then respond to a distorted version of what is truly happening beneath the surface. One person might say, “You are always attacking me,” while the other replies, “You never listen.” Both may be expressing their truth based on their experiences, but neither is addressing the deeper issue.

In the therapy room, practical morality encourages each person to clarify their communication. This doesn’t mean striving for perfection; it means simplifying your message.

Express what you mean sooner.

Ask for what you want without framing it as an accusation.

Describe what hurts you without vilifying the other person.

Set your boundaries before bitterness sets in.

Apologise without turning the apology into self-condemnation.

Listen without preparing your defence constantly.

Shift your focus from trying to win an argument to understanding the underlying pattern.

That is where real change begins.

This approach also helps you avoid a common trap: changing only as a tactic. Someone might think, “If I become calmer, they may soften.” While that can happen, using calmness as a tool to achieve a result can breed resentment if the desired outcome doesn’t come quickly enough.

The deeper reason to cultivate calmness is that it benefits you. The deeper reason, to be honest, is that honesty is good for you. The deeper reason to set boundaries is that they benefit your well-being. The deeper reason to take accountability is that accountability is healthy.

The relationship may improve as a result. In fact, it often does. However, this change should not solely rely on the other person's acknowledgement. Otherwise, it becomes mere bargaining, not true growth.

That’s a hard truth, but it is also liberating. You evolve into the person you need to be because it's better for you to be that person. It's advantageous to be someone who speaks the truth, listens compassionately, repairs misunderstandings, says no without resentment, and loves freely without control.

If the other person meets you at this level, the relationship has a chance to thrive. If they don’t, you still benefit and grow.

This represents the purest form of self-interest. It doesn’t diminish the importance of the relationship; rather, it makes your personal growth less dependent on the other person's approval.

This is particularly important in couples' work because each person often waits for the other to make the first move. They might think, “I will soften when they stop criticising,” or “I will listen when they stop getting defensive.” Others might say, “I will trust when they prove it,” or “I will try when they admit they were wrong.” While each stance may feel emotionally valid, if both individuals wait for the other to become safe first, the old patterns continue.

Someone has to change the dynamics. This doesn’t mean they are to blame for everything, nor does it suggest they are the weaker ones or that they are surrendering. Rather, it’s about choosing agency over a deadlock.

The question then becomes: “What is the next move I can make that benefits me, is honest to the relationship, and is more likely to invite the best possible response from the other person?” This question is subtle yet significant. You are not responsible for forcing a response; your responsibility lies in the quality of your invitation.

Sometimes the best available response may be little more than that. Sometimes, the other person can’t meet you where you are. At times, making a clean break may reveal the limits of the relationship rather than salvaging it. However, even that knowledge is useful. Reality is better than fantasy; a painful truth is preferable to years spent in a managed illusion.

When you stop trying to control the other person, you often gain greater clarity. You start to see what they can offer, what they cannot, where they are willing to go, and where they are defensive. You recognise where you have been asking poorly and where you have been seeking something from the wrong person.

This clarity can be painful, but it provides you with solid ground. And having a solid ground is kinder than living in confusion.

The therapeutic version of this principle is not complicated; it is simply challenging to embody.

Instead of asking, “How do I change them?” ask: “Who do I need to become so that my next move is clean?”

“Clean” does not mean soft. It does not mean pleasant or endlessly patient. “Clean” means being honest, adult, reality-based, and aligned with the best possible outcome.

Sometimes the clean move might be tenderness. Other times, it could be the truth. Sometimes it means repairing, refusing, making one more attempt, or finally deciding to stop.

Regardless of its form, the clean move comes from you standing as a full adult rather than trying to manage the other person into saving you from your own discomfort. That is the essence of the work: not control or collapse, but agency.

Ultimately, agency represents one of the deepest forms of love you can bring into a relationship. When you stop making the other person responsible for your entire emotional state, you become available to meet them as they are, respond as your true self, and choose your next move with both eyes open.
