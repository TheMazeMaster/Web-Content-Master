# 2. The Wrong Self

## Most people do not put themselves first. They put their defence first.

Most people are not as selfish as they believe. They are not calmly, clearly, or wisely acting in their own best interest. Instead, they are reacting, defending, avoiding, or trying to escape feelings of shame, fear, guilt, grief, exposure, rejection, or helplessness. They often label these reactions as self-interest, but that is not always the case. Sometimes, it is just a frightened part of them taking control.

This is important because the phrase "put yourself first" is only helpful if you understand which version of yourself you are referring to. There are multiple selves at play. There is the adult self, the future self, and the honest self, those that can slow down and consider consequences. But there are also the wounded self, the defensive self, the proud self, the impulsive self, the ashamed self, the frightened self, the controlling self, the child self, and the self that seeks to win, hide, punish, perform, collapse, or be rescued.

So, when you say, "I need to put myself first," the essential question is: which self?

Not every part of you is qualified to lead. This is not an insult; it's simply a fact. Your fear may recognise danger, but it should not dictate your entire life. Your anger may identify injustice, but it should not drive every word you speak. Your shame may highlight feelings of exposure, but it should not determine your worth. Your pain may indicate that something needs care, but it should not dictate every decision in your relationships. Your ego may try to protect you, but it often safeguards your image at the expense of truth.

These parts of ourselves are not evil or useless; they usually try to help. The problem is that they often don’t understand the consequences of their actions. They focus on immediate threats, discomfort, insults, and opportunities to avoid feeling small. However, they fail to see what follows next. They overlook how repeated defensive behaviours can harm trust, what children and partners learn from our actions, and what the other person’s nervous system records. They don’t recognise the type of person you are becoming by allowing these parts to take control.

The misguided self often speaks with great confidence. It claims, “I’m just being honest,” when in reality, it is being cruel. It insists, “I’m setting a boundary,” when it is actually withdrawing to punish. It asserts, “I’m protecting my peace,” while it avoids facing a necessary truth. It declares, “I’m not letting anyone walk all over me,” even though it treats every request as an attack. It says, “That’s just who I am,” when it really means, “I do not want to be challenged.”

This is how selfishness disguises itself. It uses adult language; words like boundaries, honesty, self-care, peace, standards, respect, freedom, and independence. However, the underlying energy reveals the truth. A genuine boundary is clear, while a fake boundary contains punishment. Real honesty includes respect, whereas fake honesty is aggressive. True self-care restores you, while fake self-care avoids responsibility. Authentic peace can confront reality, while false peace requires everyone else to stop being inconvenient.

This is why you cannot judge a move solely by its outer appearance. Silence can represent mature restraint or serve as punishment. A "no" can function as a clear boundary, or it can embody control. An apology can be a genuine attempt at repair or simply a way to end discomfort. A gift can express love or serve as a hidden invoice. A calm voice can indicate regulation, or it might conceal superiority dressed as control. A request for space can signify wisdom or be a form of avoidance.

The deeper question to consider is: what is this part of you trying to achieve? Is it attempting to build something, or is it trying to escape? Is it moving toward connection, or managing a threat? Is it seeking to tell the truth or simply protect the image of being right? Is it trying to love, or is it looking for a reaction?

This is where compassion and accountability must coexist. Every action is targeted at some good, even if it becomes messy. For instance, a controlling person may aim for safety, while an avoidant person may seek peace. An angry person might be striving for dignity, and a people-pleaser may want a sense of belonging. A critical person might aim for improvement, while a jealous person may seek reassurance. Finally, a withdrawing person might seek protection, and a blaming person may be attempting to relieve shame.

While these aims may be understandable, safety is good, peace is good, dignity is good, belonging is good, reassurance is good, protection is good, and relief is understandable, the means of achieving them can still be damaging. You may seek safety and resort to control. You might desire peace and choose avoidance. You may want dignity and respond with aggression. You could long for belonging and engage in people-pleasing. You may seek reassurance and turn to interrogation. You might desire protection and create distance. Finally, you could want relief and revert to blame.

You can hold respect for a person without agreeing with their behaviour. It's possible to say, “I understand why this action makes sense,” while also stating, “This action is too costly.” Making this distinction is important. If you only criticise the behaviour, you overlook the human being behind it. Conversely, if you only acknowledge the person, you might excuse the harm caused. A mature perspective encompasses both: it recognises the intentions with compassion while assessing the actions honestly.

Many individuals struggle with this concept because they equate being understood with being excused. However, understanding someone's behaviour does not eliminate consequences. A person may have valid reasons for being defensive; they may have a history of pain or betrayal, have grown up in an environment where honesty was unsafe or their needs were overlooked, or may carry a history of pain or betrayal. This background is significant and deserves compassion, but it doesn’t justify the ongoing use of defensiveness as a primary response.

Over time, a protective behaviour that once served you can start to limit you. What began as a shield can turn into a wall, and that wall can become a prison. Eventually, this confinement might feel like part of your identity, leading people to say, “This is just who I am.” However, that’s often not accurate; it reflects a coping mechanism. Fortunately, adaptations can be revised.

This is the hopeful aspect: the parts of ourselves we consider ‘wrong’ aren’t inherently the enemy. They are simply outdated strategies. They may have provided safety, acceptance, usefulness, alertness, invisibility, or strength at one point. However, the crucial question now shifts from “Why did this make sense?” to “Does this still serve the life I want to create?”

Proper self-interest begins to reveal itself not by shaming the defensive part of yourself, nor by pretending to be above it, or by resenting the wounded part of you for trying to survive. Instead, it manifests when you refuse to let the least mature part of you dictate your next move.

The frightened self may voice concerns, but the adult self must make the final decision. The wounded self can express feelings, but the future self must be considered. The angry self can provide valuable information, but it is the wise self that chooses how to express those feelings. The child self may have needs, but it is the adult self that bears responsibility for addressing them appropriately.

It's important to stop letting every inner state become an automatic guide. A feeling is not inherently a command, a fear is not automatically a prophecy, an urge does not equate to wisdom, a defence doesn't guarantee protection, and a desire is not always in your best interest.

Sometimes, what you want in the moment can actually trap you. You may want to send a message, prove a point, check someone’s phone, shut down, bring up the past, say something hurtful just because it's true, agree to keep someone else happy, or make them feel what you felt. However, wanting something does not mean it's beneficial for you.

Bad self-interest asks, “What am I driven to do?” while mature self-interest asks, “What are the consequences of continuing to be the kind of person who does this?”

This question carries weight because you’re not merely choosing an action; you are shaping your identity. Every truth you avoid makes avoidance easier, while every truth you embrace makes honesty more accessible. Every act of courage fosters more courage, while every moment spent blaming reinforces blame as a default response. Conversely, every moment of accountability strengthens your adult self.

This is not about perfection; perfection is just another trap. The false self thrives on perfection because it provides ammunition. If you cannot be perfect, it labels you a failure. If others cannot achieve perfection, it is deemed unsafe. Perfection keeps the nervous system preoccupied, creating impossible standards and then using failure to justify defensiveness.

The goal is not perfection. The goal is to cultivate genuine leadership within yourself. It requires a bit more pause, a bit more honesty, a bit more awareness of consequences, and a greater ability to ask, “Which part of me is trying to lead right now?”

That one question can transform your life. Once you recognise the false self clearly, you no longer have to follow it blindly. You can acknowledge the defence mechanism without giving it control. You can say, “I understand why you want to protect me, but this approach will come at too high a cost.”

This is not self-rejection; this is self-leadership.

Self-leadership is the foundation of true self-interest. Putting yourself first correctly does not mean indulging every part of you. It means organising yourself around what is genuinely beneficial. It’s not about what’s merely soothing, familiar, ego-protective, conflict-avoiding, or momentarily advantageous.

The false self seeks to survive in the moment, while the mature self aims to build a meaningful life. That is the difference. Once you recognise this distinction, your understanding of selfishness shifts. The question is no longer, “Am I putting myself first?” The more insightful question is, “Which self am I putting first?”

That question does not shame you; it grounds you. It helps you identify where the steering wheel is.

If the wrong version of yourself has been in control, that does not make you a bad person. It simply means that your internal system needs to be updated. A younger part of you may have taken charge in the past because it needed to, but now your adult self can step in, not to destroy that younger part, but to guide it.

The wrong self not only harms others; it also harms you. It keeps you living smaller than you are capable of. It causes you to fight battles that may not exist. It leads you to protect wounds that need healing. It makes you prioritise relief over growth, confusing mere survival with truly living.

Putting yourself first means you stop allowing this part to control everything. You become more than just your defences. You move beyond your knee-jerk reactions and old adaptations. You transform into someone who can pause, observe, make choices, and take action.

This is not a sign of weakness; it is power returning to where it rightfully belongs.
