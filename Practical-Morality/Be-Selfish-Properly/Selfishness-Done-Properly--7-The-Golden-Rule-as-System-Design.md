# 7. The Golden Rule as System Design

## Treating others well builds the kind of world it is better for you to live in.

Treat Others as You Would Like to be Treated

Most people are familiar with the phrase. It's simple enough to sound childish, obvious enough to ignore, and well-known enough to lose its impact. It often comes across as something from a school assembly, a religious lesson, or a poster on a wall that goes unnoticed.

However, beneath its simplicity lies a powerful message.

The Golden Rule is not merely a moral guideline; it is a system design principle. It doesn't just say, "Be nice." It conveys a deeper idea: "Act in a way that helps create the kind of world you truly want to live in." This concept is much more significant.

If you wish to live in a world where people tell the truth, be someone who fosters an environment where truth is safe to express. If you desire a world where people can admit their mistakes, do not punish every confession. If you want people to respect boundaries, make sure to respect theirs. If you hope for a world where people listen to one another, listen without turning every discussion into a courtroom. If you want a space where people can be human, refrain from demanding perfection before you show them kindness.

This is where the concept of goodness becomes practical.

You are not treating others well simply to appear saintly, nor are you doing it because you are naïve or want to be exploited. You treat others well because every interaction shapes the environment you have to live in.

The way you treat people teaches them what to expect from you. It also informs your own nervous system about the kind of world you believe exists. If you see everyone as a threat, you will find danger everywhere. If you turn every disagreement into a battle, your relationships will become conflict zones. If you use shame to control others, don’t be surprised when they start to hide from you. If you punish honesty, don't expect people to be candid with you in the future.

This isn’t a mystery; it’s a consequence.

The Golden Rule urges you to recognise that you are not merely going through life; you are actively contributing to it. Not all of it, not perfectly, and not alone. But remember, you are never just a passive observer. Each repeated action is a vote for the kind of environment you are creating.

This is especially pertinent in close relationships. If you want more warmth, how you respond to offered warmth matters. If you want more honesty, how you react when honesty is difficult is crucial. If you wish for mutual respect, your own respect must endure through frustration. If you want your partner to show vulnerability, how you handle their softness is significant: whether you meet it with care or use it against them later.

People do not open up in unsafe environments. They may comply, perform, and say the right things, but that doesn't equate to genuine openness. Real openness requires a space where honesty can thrive without fear of repercussions.

That is why treating others well is ultimately in your own best interest.

Not because it guarantees that others will treat you well in return. That would be too simple. Some people won't reciprocate. Some might take your kindness for granted and still behave carelessly. Others may respond to your honesty with defensiveness, and some may lack the maturity to respond positively to what you offer.

However, treating people well still matters because it maintains your integrity. It keeps you aligned with your values and prevents you from becoming the kind of person you would not want to meet. It also provides you with clearer insights, as you are not clouding the situation with aggression, manipulation, or contempt.

Goodness is not a guarantee of reward; it is a commitment to remaining uncorrupted while you seek the truth. This is a more profound kind of goodness; less childish and less transactional. It's about being adult, grounded, and free.

Many people get stuck in the mindset of asking, “Why should I treat them well if they aren’t treating me well?” This is a valid question, but it requires a clearer answer. You don’t treat others well because they have earned your kindness; you do it because your behaviour belongs to you.

You can treat someone well and still say no. You can treat someone well and still leave. You can treat someone well and still establish firm boundaries. You can treat someone well and still refuse to engage in a damaging conversation. You can treat someone well and still protect yourself.

Treating someone well does not mean giving them what they want. It means you do not compromise your own integrity in response to their behaviour. You do not become cruel because they are careless. You do not become dishonest because they are defensive. You do not become controlling because they are unpredictable. You do not become contemptuous because they are difficult.

This is not about them deserving your best; it is about you belonging to yourself.

This is one of the fundamental truths of practical morality: the way you treat others is also the way you train yourself. If you repeatedly choose contempt, it becomes more available to you. If you choose honesty, patience, or manipulation repeatedly, those behaviours become more ingrained as well. You are not only shaping the relationship; you are shaping the person you are becoming.

That is why the Golden Rule is not merely sentimental; it is a disciplined practice. It calls for you to act in accordance with the world you want to live in, rather than the world you fear. It asks you to cultivate the environment you hope to thrive under. If you want trust, actively exhibit trustworthiness. If you want respect, demonstrate respect. If you want understanding, practice curiosity. If you seek repair, hold yourself accountable and cultivate enough mercy so that people can be honest without fear of destruction.

This does not mean ignoring reality; discernment is essential. You should not keep planting seeds in soil that refuses to bear fruit. You should not continue to offer openness to someone who consistently uses it to harm you. You cannot pretend that a destructive person is safe simply because you want to be good. Practical morality is not blind; it acknowledges consequences, requires distance, and demands the wisdom to stop granting access when it is being abused.

Even distance can be clean. A clean distance communicates, “I cannot stay close to this, but I do not have to dehumanise you to protect myself.” A clean boundary states, “This cannot continue, but I do not need to punish you to make that clear.” A clean no expresses, “I am not available for this, and I do not need to harbour hatred to give myself permission.”

This kind of emotional cleanliness is important. It protects you from the false belief that you must harden yourself to feel safe. You do not need to become hard; you need to be clear. Hardness may offer temporary protection, but it often comes at a high cost. It can block the meaningful connections you may still want with people who are safe enough to engage with you.

The Golden Rule, when understood as system design, does not require you to be endlessly open. Instead, it asks you to take responsibility for the kind of signal you send into the world. It asks you to recognise that your presence influences the environment around you. Your tone has consequences. Your reactions shape people's behaviour. Your patterns create conditions.

Some people make honesty easier to engage with.

Some people make honesty feel dangerous.

Some people facilitate repair in relationships.

Some people make repair feel humiliating.

Some people welcome vulnerability.

Some people make vulnerability feel regrettable.

The key question is: What kind of person are you becoming to be around? This is not about being performative, creating an image, or establishing a brand. It’s about the actual reality of your closest relationships.

Are you someone people can tell the truth to?

Are you someone people can disappoint without fear of destruction?

Are you someone with whom people can engage in repair?

Are you someone people can say no to?

Are you someone whose love allows another person to remain authentic?

These questions matter because they ground morality in lived practice. They move it from theory to the everyday moments, like in the kitchen, the bedroom, the car, through text messages, during difficult conversations, moments after someone admits something, or just before you react.

The Golden Rule does not demand perfection. It encourages you to become more conscious of what you are creating in your interactions.

If you treat others as objects, you contribute to building a world of objects.

If you treat others as enemies, you help create a world filled with hostility.

If you treat others as children, you foster a world of dependence.

If you treat others as threats, you build a world focused on defence.

If you treat others as whole people, you help create a world where everyone has a better chance of being their authentic selves.

That is the point. You are not responsible for how everyone else responds. You cannot control the entire situation, nor are you tasked with improving the world by force. However, you are accountable for the quality of your own participation, and that is where true power lies.

The Golden Rule is often taught as a moral guideline for the sake of goodness. While there is truth to that, there is also a practical aspect. Treating others well is not separate from taking care of yourself; it is one way to prioritise your well-being. The world you help create is the world you ultimately have to inhabit.

If you want a world filled with more trust, do not reward yourself for mistrustful behaviour.

If you desire a softer world, do not penalise softness when it manifests.

If you seek greater honesty, do not make honesty more costly than concealment.

If you wish for a world with more love, do not turn love into a test.

This perspective is not naive; it is grounded in reality.

Reality keeps track of both failures and repairs. It notes the positive signals, the clear "no," the honest exchanges, and the moments when you could have chosen punishment but instead opted for clarity. It acknowledges instances when you could have exerted control but chose truth instead. It recognises moments when you could have walked away but decided to engage instead.

These moments matter. They are significant. They are how change occurs at the human level, not all at once, nor everywhere, and certainly not perfectly, but in the specific spaces you interact with: in your home, your relationships, your children's memories, your friendships, and your work. They also resonate within the private landscape of your own mind.

"Treat others as you would like to be treated" is not just a simple rule; it is a blueprint. It advises you to construct the conditions you would want to live in and to start with the next person in front of you.
