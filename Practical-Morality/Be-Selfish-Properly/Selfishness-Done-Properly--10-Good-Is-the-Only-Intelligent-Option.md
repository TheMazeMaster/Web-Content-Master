# 10. Good Is the Only Intelligent Option

## Be truly selfish. Go the extra mile and think of yourself tomorrow.

So you see, there is only one option: be selfish.

But not in a cheap, blind way. Not like a child grabbing for comfort. Not like a frightened person trying to control the room. Not like someone using self-care language to avoid responsibility. Not like someone who confuses cruelty with honesty, withdrawal with boundaries, people-pleasing with kindness, or winning with success.

Be properly selfish. Be deeply selfish. Go the extra mile in your selfishness and think of yourself tomorrow.

Consider the version of you who has to wake up inside the choices you are making right now. Think about the version of you who has to live with the trust you either built or damaged. Reflect on the version of you who has to repair relationships, calm your nervous system, face the truth, carry the lie, explain the pattern, or sit in the silence that your repeated actions have created.

That is the crux of the matter.

Practical morality begins when self-interest matures enough to include consequences.

It’s easy to be selfish for five minutes. Anyone can do that. You can say something hurtful for a brief release. You can hide the truth to buy yourself time. You can say yes to avoid discomfort. You can control the other person to calm your own fears. You can punish them to feel powerful over your pain. You can avoid difficult conversations because avoidance seems peaceful from a distance.

But then what? What happens next? What do your actions teach about the relationship? What do they instil in you? What do they teach the other person to expect? And what impact do they have on trust, closeness, self-respect, truth, freedom, and peace?

This is where the bill arrives.

This is why goodness is not merely a decorative idea. Goodness is not there to make you appear nice or to encourage you to be soft, obedient, passive, or endlessly available. Goodness is not a costume worn by those who have no needs. It is not self-erasure dressed up in a smile.

Goodness is intelligent alignment with reality. It is what occurs when you stop pretending that the moment is separate from the future.

A lie might help you escape the moment, but it makes the truth more costly later. Cruelty might give you a sense of strength, but it makes rebuilding safety harder. Avoidance might help you dodge discomfort, but it gives discomfort more power. People-pleasing might maintain approval, but it can also foster resentment. Control might provide a sense of safety, but it turns the other person into someone to be managed rather than truly met.

None of that is good for you.

Here’s the twist: the bad move is not only harmful to the other person; it is detrimental to you as well. It keeps you unstable. It makes you dependent on control. It maintains your conflict with reality. It forces you to live within systems you then complain about. It makes you wait for others to change before you can stand confidently in your own truth.

Proper selfishness refuses that deal. It says, “I will not betray my future for a moment of relief.” It says, “I will not become less honest because the truth is uncomfortable.” It says, “I will not become cruel simply because I am hurt.” It says, “I will not label people-pleasing as love when it is actually fear.” It says, “I will not call control care when it is really anxiety.” It says, “I will not use another person as a prop to stabilise what I have not yet faced in myself.”

That is not moral decoration; that is self-protection at the deepest level.

When you choose honesty, you are not just doing something noble; you are making your life more stable. When you embrace accountability, you are not only giving someone else a chance to repair things; you are making yourself stronger. When you set a clear boundary, you are not only protecting yourself from others; you are also protecting love from turning into resentment. When you cooperate, you are not just being kind; you are refusing to waste life by turning every difficult moment into a battle.

This is why the list matters.

Cooperation. Honesty. Boundaries. Trust. Repair. Maturity. Agency. Discernment. Patience. Reciprocity. Generosity. Self-respect. Forgiveness. Accountability. Adaptation. Humility. Wisdom. Courage. Compassion. Regard. Self-control. Integrity. Peace. Connection. Survival.

These virtues are not separate ideals floating around in a moral display cabinet. They represent different facets of mature self-interest.

Honesty is self-interest because lies create instability in your life. Boundaries are self-interest because self-abandonment leads to resentment. Repair is self-interest because unresolved conflicts create distance. Accountability is self-interest because avoiding responsibility keeps you weak. Compassion is self-interest because understanding people clearly allows you to make wise choices. Discernment is self-interest because not everyone is trustworthy. Patience is self-interest because forcing outcomes can cause damage. Courage is self-interest because fear is a poor long-term leader. Integrity is self-interest because you must live with yourself when no one else is watching. Peace is self-interest because internal conflict is costly. Connection is self-interest because humans do not thrive in isolation. Survival is self-interest because any organism that fails to adapt ultimately suffers.

This is not about being good for the sake of receiving praise. It is about understanding the dynamics of life.

Life persists. Relationships leave a lasting impact. Our bodies store experiences. Children observe and remember. Patterns repeat; trust can grow or diminish. Repair can enhance or diminish connections. Avoidance compounds issues. Every action teaches something, and every repeated action shapes the world around us.

So, the question becomes painfully simple:

What kind of world are you helping to create?

Not in a grand, abstract way. Start small, focus on your immediate surroundings. What kind of world are you creating in your home? In your relationships? In your friendships? In the atmosphere your children experience? How do people feel when they share the truth with you? How does conflict unfold around you? How does your own body feel when you are alone with yourself?

That’s where morality becomes tangible.

Not in theory. Not in slogans. Not in merely saying the right thing.

It’s in the lived experience of your presence.

Are you someone people can be honest with? Are you able to hear pain without immediately getting defensive? Can you say no clearly? Can you apologise without feeling overwhelmed? Can you give without expecting anything in return? Can you endure hurt without resorting to cruelty? Can you love without needing to control?

These questions are not meant to make you feel bad.

They are there to help you regain your sense of agency. The goal is not perfection; perfection is merely another trap. The focus is on direction and making cleaner choices. It’s about becoming someone you can trust more often, especially when situations become challenging.

You will make mistakes. Everyone does. You may react from a less authentic part of yourself. You might try to protect the moment, avoid the truth, say hurtful things, please others at the cost of your own feelings, or turn someone into a problem to be solved instead of viewing them as a person to connect with. You might confuse fear with wisdom.

However, these missteps do not make your efforts inauthentic. They make your work essential.

Practical morality does not mean you will always choose correctly; it’s about the commitment to return when you don’t get it right. Return to the truth. Return to making amends. Return to assuming responsibility. Return to the future self you want to become. Always return to the question, “What is this creating?”

That question serves as a compass, not a rulebook. A rulebook might be easier. It would provide clear instructions like “Always do this. Never do that.” But human life is much more complex than that. Sometimes, the loving response is to be gentle; at other times, it is to be firm. Sometimes it means staying, while other times it means leaving. Occasionally, it involves speaking up, but often, it’s best to wait until you can express yourself without causing harm. Sometimes, it is about giving; other times, it’s about recognising that you cannot give cleanly.

The approach may vary, but the guiding principles do not. Choose the action that aligns with truth, consequence, dignity, connection, and the future you wish to inhabit.

That is the path of adulthood, and it is profoundly selfish in the best sense. By living this way, you gain more of what you truly desired all along. It may not come from the exact person you hoped for, nor within your preferred timeframe, and it may not be free of grief. But you will find more truth, more peace, more self-respect, more clarity, and more genuine connections where they are possible. You will also experience greater freedom from the old patterns that kept dragging you back into the same situations, just in different forms.

You stop needing to win every moment because you're no longer playing just for that moment. You stop needing to control every person because you're more willing to accept reality. You stop needing to be seen as good because you're more interested in being genuine. You no longer wait for others to be perfect before you take responsibility for your own actions.

This is actualisation in simple terms, not becoming some shiny, finished product, floating above your wounds, or being endlessly calm, wise, and untouched. Actualisation is more grounded than that. It represents a shift from reaction to choice, from defence to agency, from impulse to consequence, from using people to genuinely meeting them. It transforms hidden agendas into clean giving, shifts focus from winning the moment to building a meaningful life.

This is why choosing good is the only intelligent option. Every other choice eventually turns against you.

Cruelty isolates you. Dishonesty destabilises you. Control exhausts you. Avoidance shrinks you. People-pleasing hides you. Blame traps you. Contempt hardens you. Self-abandonment leaves you empty. Short-term selfishness betrays the very self it claims to serve.

Goodness, when understood properly, does the opposite. It organises you. It provides you with stability. It enables you to move in ways you can respect. It allows other people to be real without making their reality the ruler of your soul. It lets you care without losing yourself. It allows you to protect yourself without becoming hardened. It enables you to pursue connection without using the other person as a means to an end. It allows you to exist in your own life with less hiding.

This approach is not soft. It is strong. It is sane. It embodies proper selfishness.

So yes, prioritise yourself. But do so fully. Don’t stop at the self that seeks relief, desires to win, avoids shame, dodges discomfort, tries to control the environment, or maintains peace at all costs. Go further.

Be more selfish than that. Consider your future, your character, your relationships, your body, your peace, and the person you become when no one limits you. Reflect on the world that your repeated actions create around you.

Be so deeply self-interested that you become honest. Be so deeply self-interested that you establish boundaries. Be so deeply self-interested that you stop using people. Be so deeply self-interested that you learn to repair. Be so deeply self-interested that you give generously or admit when you cannot.

Be so deeply self-interested that you choose cooperation when possible and maintain distance when necessary. Be so deeply self-interested that you refuse to become the kind of person your fears would turn you into.

This is practical morality, not goodness as performance, weakness, or self-erasure. It is goodness as the most intelligent way to navigate a world that remembers your actions.

So, there is only one option: Be truly selfish. Go the extra mile. Think of your future self.
