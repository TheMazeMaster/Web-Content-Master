# 9. Giving Freely, Without the Hidden Invoice

## Give freely, or do not call it giving.

Giving can be one of the areas where people often become confused.

At first glance, it seems simple: you give because you care, you help because you love, and you offer because someone matters to you. You make the effort, hold the space, carry the weight, do favours, stay patient, forgive, support, listen, provide, reassure, and show up. There is beauty in that.

True giving is one of the purest forms of love. It conveys the message, “I have something I can offer, and I choose to offer it.” It is not forced, nor is it extracted or performed for applause. It is not a bargaining chip hidden beneath kindness; it comes from a place within you that is full enough that the act of giving itself is part of the joy.

However, not all giving is pure. Some giving comes with a hidden agenda. You may say yes, but a quiet part of you is keeping score. You offer help, but deep down, you are expecting recognition. You sacrifice, expecting the other person to understand, without being told, how much you gave up. You tell yourself you are being loving, but later you feel angry because the recognition never came.

The issue is not that you want something in return. Wanting appreciation, care, effort, tenderness, respect, or reciprocity is not wrong; those things matter. The problem arises when you do not clearly express your needs. Instead, you give as if it is free while secretly treating it like a contract that the other person never saw or signed.

Then resentment sets in.

Resentment often sounds like this: “After everything I’ve done for you.” This statement usually reveals the hidden agenda behind your giving. It shows that the act was not as free as it appeared. It may have been sincere and stemmed from love, but it was mixed with expectation, fear, pressure, self-neglect, or the hope that if you gave enough, the other person would finally become who you needed them to be.

This is a painful bargain that hurts both parties. The giver feels unseen and used, while the receiver feels trapped, guilty, or confused. The relationship becomes complicated because love is no longer flowing freely. Each act of giving begins to carry emotional baggage.

This is why giving needs honesty.

If you want something, ask. If you need something, name it. If you cannot give freely, take a moment to pause. If saying yes will lead to resentment, it may not be a pure yes.

This does not mean you should only give when it is easy. Love often requires effort. Parenting, partnerships, friendships, and care all involve effort. Sometimes, you will do things you would not naturally choose to do because someone matters to you. That doesn’t automatically make it unhealthy; after all, a life shared with others will require something from you.

The question is whether the choice of giving is clear.

There’s a difference between sacrifice and self-abandonment. Sacrifice can be a conscious decision. It says, “This matters enough that I am willing to give something up.” It is owned and truthful; it doesn't pretend and doesn’t later criticise the other person for receiving what you have chosen to give.

Self-abandonment, on the other hand, is different. It says yes because saying no feels dangerous. It gives because being needed feels safer than being honest. It maintains peace because conflict feels unbearable. It performs acts of goodwill because seeking approval feels necessary. Then later, the neglected self begins to protest.

This protest often manifests in indirect ways, such as irritability, coldness, scorekeeping, sarcasm, emotional withdrawal, sudden anger, or quiet contempt. The person may still be “giving,” but the warmth has vanished. The act remains, but love has turned into obligation.

This is not practical morality; it is confusion dressed in a kind face.

Proper self-interest does not ask you to stop giving; it encourages you to give from a purer place. You shouldn’t be giving to gain control, earn love, avoid rejection, maintain an image, or establish reasons why someone owes you. Instead, give because you truly choose to give.

Give because it aligns with who you want to be. Give because the act of giving itself is part of the life you wish to live. Give because love that is freely expressed is beneficial for you.

That last point is important. Giving can be rewarding, not in a simplistic, transactional way, and not because you expect something in return, but because a life devoid of generosity becomes small. If you only protect, calculate, withhold, and measure, you might evade being taken advantage of, but you could also lose touch with the part of yourself that wants to contribute something good to the world.

The solution is not to become guarded and ungenerous. Rather, it is to become clean. Clean giving has boundaries. It does not pretend to have infinite capacity; it does not say yes to everything. It does not confuse love with availability, and it does not seek to be perceived as good at the expense of being truthful.

Clean giving can articulate:

“I can do this.”

“I cannot do that.”

“I want to help.”

“I don’t have the capacity.”

“I am choosing this freely.”

“I realise I am starting to feel resentful, so I need to be honest.”

This is not less loving; it is more loving because it safeguards the relationship from the burden of unspoken expectations.

People-pleasing often masquerades as love because it evades immediate discomfort. However, it is rarely as generous as it appears. It presents a false version of yourself and leads others to believe something is acceptable when it is not. It avoids the short-term discomfort of honesty, resulting in the long-term cost of resentment.

If you continuously say yes when you mean no, the other person will not truly get to know you; they will only become familiar with your performance. Later, you might blame them for not recognising the truth you concealed. That is unfair.

It may be understandable, especially if you learned early on that your needs were seen as inconvenient, dangerous, or excessive. However, understandable does not equate to healthy or honest. If you desire a relationship built on truth, you must stop making the other person guess where the truth lies.

This is where proper self-interest and love intersect. It is in your best interest to give genuinely because authentic giving does not harm you. It prevents your love from turning into resentment and stops the other person from feeling responsible for deciphering your limits. You shouldn't have to keep a silent tally of everything you’ve done for them.

A hidden ledger can damage the relationship. The other person may sense that your kindness carries pressure; they might feel guilty for accepting your help. They could perceive that your generosity comes with strings attached, making them feel obligated. This type of giving does not foster closeness; it creates obligation, which is not the same as love.

True love allows for freedom. That doesn’t mean there aren’t commitments; reliability, mutual care, and commitment are important. But freedom is crucial as well. If your giving is only comfortable when the other person responds exactly as you wish, then it was not genuinely free; it was a transactional move.

This is difficult because everyone wants to matter, to be appreciated, and to have their efforts recognised. There is nothing wrong with wanting that. The key is not to suppress your needs within your acts of giving.

If you want appreciation, express it. If you desire more mutual effort, communicate that. If you feel lonely, let it be known. If you are tired of shouldering responsibilities alone, say so.

This approach is much cleaner than giving until you become bitter and then presenting your resentment as proof of their failure. A hidden ledger turns love into a courtroom. Clean giving keeps love out in the open.

This clarity is especially important in long-term relationships because daily life is filled with small acts of giving: making meals, earning money, cleaning, listening, waiting, driving, remembering, supporting, taking turns, managing the household, caregiving, and carrying emotional burdens. None of these acts is trivial when repeated over the years.

If they are not discussed clearly, they can become loaded. One person may feel they are giving everything, while the other may be unaware of the internal costs. One might believe their efforts demonstrate love, while the other may perceive it as control or criticism. One may wait for gratitude, while the other waits for openness.

This is where clear communication becomes essential. No need for dramatic speeches, accusations, or phrases like “after everything I do.” Just speak the truth:

"I want to give, but I'm starting to feel overwhelmed."

"I realise I've been saying yes and then feeling resentful."

"I need us to discuss how this is shared."

"I want to do this out of love, not obligation."

That kind of honesty protects giving from decay. Because giving can decay; it can start as love and morph into duty, generosity into resentment, care into control, and devotion into identity. You can become so attached to being the giver that you no longer know how to receive, ask, rest, or allow yourself to be supported.

That is not noble; it creates imbalance. Proper self-interest encourages you to remain part of your own love. If your giving is depleting you, it is not genuine. If your sacrifice breeds bitterness, it needs to be addressed. If your kindness requires you to disappear, it is no longer kindness to the relationship; it becomes a gradual distortion.

The goal of giving in relationships is not about achieving a perfect, mechanical balance. Relationships are not spreadsheets. There will be times when one person has to carry more than the other due to circumstances like illness, stress, grief, parenting, work, crises, recovery, and change. In these moments, love sometimes expresses itself as, “I have more capacity right now, so I will take on more.”

This can be a beautiful thing. However, even in these situations, it’s crucial that this carrying is rooted in truth. It requires mutual consent, awareness, and the space to express, “This is becoming heavy.” Without this, the person who is carrying more may gradually turn their goodwill into a burden.

True giving doesn’t come with strings attached. It doesn’t say, “You owe me.” Instead, it states, “This is mine to offer, and I offer it freely.” If it’s not free, it needs to be communicated truthfully. That is the essence of this discipline.

Give freely, or don't label it as giving. Ask clearly, or don’t disguise your request as a sacrifice. Say no outright, rather than agreeing and then resenting it later. Establish boundaries in your love so it remains pure.

This isn’t cold; it’s actually warmer than the alternative. When giving is honest, the recipient can fully receive it. They won’t have to fear hidden costs or search for underlying resentment behind a smile. They won’t wonder when the bill will arrive. Instead, they can genuinely feel the gift, and you can relish the act of giving it.

That is the goal, not giving as a performance, a means of control, a way to prove oneself, or a form of debt. Instead, it’s about giving as a clean expression of who you choose to be.

This type of giving is not selfless in an empty way; it includes you. It respects your limits and honours your freedom. It gives the other person dignity by not manipulating them into repayment, fostering a kind of love that can breathe.

This kind of relationship is in your best interest. A life where you can give freely, receive honestly, ask clearly, and say no straightforwardly is far better than one built on unspoken agreements and unpaid emotional debts.
