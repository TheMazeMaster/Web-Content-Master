## 7. Resistance Is Baked In

There is a part of you that may wish people could simply see themselves. If the masculine could see how it wounds the feminine when it becomes hard and dismissive. If the feminine could see how it wounds the masculine when it becomes engulfing or indirect. If the man could see the woman. If the woman could see the man. If the active could see the receptive. If the emotional could see the structured. If each side could simply understand the other, perhaps the whole thing would soften.

But resistance is baked in for a reason.

People do not resist only because they are stubborn. They resist because their current pattern is serving something. It may not be serving their highest good. It may not be serving the relationship. It may not be serving truth. But it is serving survival, identity, familiarity, power, protection, pride, attachment, or avoidance.

The masculine resists the feminine when the feminine feels like chaos. If emotion once meant danger, unpredictability, criticism, engulfment, or humiliation, then emotional openness will not feel like intimacy. It will feel like losing control. A person may say, “I just do not see the point in talking about feelings,” but underneath that may be, “I do not know how to enter that world without being swallowed, blamed, or exposed.”

The feminine resists the masculine when the masculine feels like threat. If structure once meant domination, criticism, abandonment, control, or emotional coldness, then directness will not feel like safety. It will feel like being overpowered. A person may say, “You are being harsh,” but underneath that may be, “I do not know how to stay connected when you come at me with that much edge.”

The masculine woman may resist softness because softness once cost too much. Maybe no one came. Maybe dependence was punished. Maybe vulnerability was used against her. Maybe she had to become capable before she had the luxury of being held. So when softness is invited, her nervous system does not hear, “You can rest now.” It hears, “You are about to be unsafe.”

The feminine man may resist force because force feels morally dangerous. Maybe he has seen power misused. Maybe he fears becoming like someone who hurt him. Maybe his sensitivity became his identity because the alternative looked brutal. So when assertion is required, his nervous system does not hear, “Stand up.” It hears, “If you become forceful, you will lose love or become harmful.”

This is why telling someone to “just communicate” often fails. Communication is not just a skill. It is an exposure. You are asking someone to enter the very territory their defence was designed to avoid.

You tell the masculine person, “Be vulnerable,” and they may hear, “Become weak and lose respect.”

You tell the feminine person, “Set a boundary,” and they may hear, “Risk abandonment and become selfish.”

You tell the controlling person, “Trust more,” and they may hear, “Stop protecting yourself.”

You tell the passive person, “Be direct,” and they may hear, “Become dangerous.”

Resistance is not always the enemy of growth. Sometimes it is the doorway. It shows you where the threat lives. It shows you what the person believes would happen if they changed. It shows you the hidden loyalty to an old survival strategy.

This matters because people often attack the resistance instead of understanding it. They say, “Why can’t you just open up?” or “Why can’t you just calm down?” or “Why can’t you just make a decision?” or “Why can’t you just stop controlling everything?” But the word just is often cruel. It makes the difficult thing sound simple, and it makes the person feel defective for struggling with it.

A better question is:

“What would become unsafe if you did this differently?”

If you opened up, what are you afraid would happen?

If you held the boundary, what are you afraid it would cost?

If you let someone help, what would that expose?

If you stopped chasing, what would you have to feel?

If you stopped fixing, what would you have to sit with?

If you stopped controlling, what uncertainty would you have to tolerate?

If you stopped pleasing, who might be disappointed?

These questions reveal the deeper system. The resistance is not random. It is organised around a feared consequence.

|**Pattern of Resistance**|**Surface Behaviour**|**Feared Consequence Beneath It**|**Growth Edge**|
|---|---|---|---|
|**Masculine resists vulnerability**|Fixing, dismissing, joking, silence, irritation|Shame, weakness, loss of respect, not knowing what to do|Feel without collapsing. Listen without fixing.|
|**Feminine resists boundary**|Overgiving, hinting, pleasing, resentment|Abandonment, rejection, becoming selfish, losing connection|Say no cleanly. Ask directly. Stay open without self-erasing.|
|**Masculine woman resists receiving**|Taking over, correcting, controlling, testing|Dependence, betrayal, being trapped, being unsupported|Let support in without surrendering discernment.|
|**Feminine man resists assertion**|Avoiding, softening the truth, withdrawing, over-explaining|Conflict, rejection, becoming harmful, losing love|Speak plainly. Want cleanly. Stand without attacking.|
|**Any person resists integration**|“This is just how I am.”|Loss of identity, exposure, unfamiliar responsibility|Keep the gift. Mature the defence.|

Once you see the feared consequence, compassion becomes possible. Not soft indulgence. Real compassion. The kind that understands why the pattern exists but still asks for responsibility.

You can say, “I understand why control feels safer, and it is still hurting us.”

You can say, “I understand why emotion floods you, and you still need to speak fairly.”

You can say, “I understand why directness scares you, and we still need truth in the room.”

You can say, “I understand why vulnerability feels humiliating, and I still need to know you.”

This is the balance. Without compassion, you attack the defence and make it stronger. Without responsibility, you excuse the defence and let it run the relationship.

Resistance is baked in because change is not just behavioural. It is identity-level. The masculine person who becomes emotionally open may feel like they are betraying their own idea of strength. The feminine person who becomes boundaried may feel like they are betraying their own idea of love. The capable person who receives may feel like they are betraying their survival. The gentle person who asserts may feel like they are betraying their goodness.

So the work must be respectful. You are not trying to rip someone out of their pattern. You are helping them see the cost of staying unconscious inside it.

The masculine does not need to be shamed into softness.

The feminine does not need to be mocked into strength.

The guarded do not need to be forced open.

The sensitive do not need to be hardened.

Each side needs a bridge.

A way to move without self-betrayal.

A way to grow that honours what the old pattern protected, while no longer allowing it to rule.

That is the key.

You do not destroy the old pattern.

You mature it.
