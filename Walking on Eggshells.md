# **walking-on-eggshells.md**

_When you feel like being yourself might cost you something_

---

## 1. The Subtle Shift


There’s a certain kind of tension that doesn’t always show itself clearly.

It’s not always shouting.  
It’s not always obvious conflict.

It’s quieter than that.

It shows up in the pause before you speak.  
In the edit you make mid-sentence.  
In the thing you decide isn’t worth bringing up.

You might not even call it anything at first.

You just notice you’re… adjusting.

A little more careful than you used to be.  
A little more aware of how things might land.  
A little more selective with what you say, and how you say it.

And over time, that adjustment becomes your normal.

---

## 2. Living One Step Ahead

You start to think in ways like:

> “I’ll just leave that.”  
> “This will turn into something.”  
> “It’s not worth the hassle.”

Or sometimes the opposite:

> “I need to say this properly.”  
> “If I don’t explain it right, it’ll go wrong.”  
> “I have to be really clear here or they’ll take it the wrong way.”

Different thoughts, same pattern.

You are no longer just speaking.

You are managing the moment before it even happens.

---

## 3. The Loop You Don’t See

And this is where people usually land on the phrase:

> “I feel like I’m walking on eggshells.”

It sounds like the other person is the cause of that.

Like they:

- react too strongly
- take things the wrong way
- make it hard to just be normal

And sometimes, that’s partly true.

But if you stop there, you miss the part that actually gives you your power back.




Because the real shift isn’t just in what they do.

It’s in what _you start doing in response_.

Before anything even happens, you begin to:

- predict
- adjust
- filter
- prepare

You start living one step ahead of the interaction.

And here’s the part most people don’t see:

The more you do that, the less clear you become.



You say things slightly differently than you mean them.  
You soften things that needed to be direct.  
You wrap things in extra explanation.

Or you say nothing at all.

And now, what the other person is responding to…  
is not clean.

It’s filtered.



So they react to _that_.

Not to what you actually meant.

And when they react, it confirms your expectation:

> “See, this is exactly why I have to be careful.”

And just like that, the loop tightens.

---
## 4. How It Starts to Distort

This is the paradox of eggshells.

The more you walk carefully,  
the more unnatural the interaction becomes.

And the more unnatural it becomes,  
the more likely it is something will go wrong.

Not because either of you are trying to create a problem.

But because neither of you are now responding to something clean.



What you’re left with is a system that reacts faster than it understands.

And inside that system, both people start losing something.

You lose ease.  
You lose clarity.  
You lose the sense that you can just be yourself and be met cleanly.



Now, this doesn’t show up in one way.

It moves.

It shifts.

And depending on the moment, you might find yourself in different positions inside it.

Sometimes you’ll recognise yourself clearly in one of these.

Sometimes you’ll move between them without even noticing.



There are patterns here that repeat.

Not personalities.  
Not fixed traits.

Positions people fall into when the pressure builds and clarity drops.



You might notice, for example, that you start holding things back.

Not because you don’t care.

Because you care enough to avoid the reaction.

You think:

- “I’ll deal with it later”
- “This isn’t the right time”
- “It’ll just cause a problem”

So you let it go.

And then something else.

And then something else.

Until what you’re holding internally is far bigger than what’s actually being spoken.



Or you might notice the opposite.

You do speak.

But when it comes out, it has weight behind it.

Not just the moment, but everything that’s been building.

So it lands stronger than intended.

And now the other person isn’t responding to your point.

They’re responding to the intensity.



Or you might find yourself trying to get it exactly right.

You explain more.  
You soften more.  
You try to shape it so carefully that there’s no room for it to go wrong.

But in doing that, something strange happens.

The message gets harder to follow.

It loses its edge.  
Its clarity.  
Its centre.

And now it can be interpreted in all sorts of ways you were trying to avoid.



And sometimes, it’s not even about what you say.

It’s about what you hear.

Something gets said, and it lands bigger than it was.

You don’t just hear the words.

You hear what it _feels like it means_.

So:

> “That didn’t sit right with me”

Becomes:

> “You’ve done something wrong”

And now you’re reacting to something that wasn’t actually said.



None of these are “bad behaviours.”

They are all attempts to manage something that feels unstable.

Each one makes sense in isolation.

But together, they create a system where:

- expression becomes distorted
- reactions become amplified
- meaning becomes unreliable

And the result is the same on both sides:

> “I can’t just be myself here.”

---

## 5. The Shift Back to Solid Ground

This is where most people try to fix the other person.

If only they:

- reacted less
- listened better
- explained themselves differently

Then everything would settle.

---

But the shift doesn’t start there.

It starts somewhere much more uncomfortable, and much more empowering.

It starts with this question:

> “Am I being clear, or am I adapting to avoid something?”

---

Because the moment you start adapting before the interaction happens…

You are no longer standing on solid ground.

You are standing on prediction.

And prediction is not stable enough to build anything on.

---

The work here is not to remove all reaction.

That’s not realistic.

The work is to bring yourself back into the moment in a way that is:

- clear
- contained
- grounded

So that what you say…  
is actually what you mean.

---

## 6. What You Do Instead

And that is where things begin to change.

Not all at once.

Not perfectly.

But enough to start breaking the loop.

You don’t fix eggshells by removing the other person.

You fix it by becoming someone who can stand still _inside the moment_ without reshaping yourself to survive it.

That sounds simple.

It isn’t.

Because the moment you stop adapting, the system reacts.

---

If you’ve been holding things back, speaking cleanly will feel risky.

If you’ve been pushing, softening and containing will feel like losing impact.

If you’ve been over-explaining, saying less will feel exposed.

If you’ve been misreading, slowing down will feel uncertain.

---

That reaction is not a sign you’re doing it wrong.

It’s a sign you’ve stopped playing your usual position in the loop.

---

This is where the work becomes very practical.

Not theory. Not insight.

What you _actually do_ when the moment happens.

---

Start with this.

You don’t need to fix everything.

You need to pick one position you recognise in yourself…  
and run a cleaner version of it.

---

If you tend to hold things back…

You don’t swing to saying everything.

You bring one thing forward, clearly, without loading it.

Not:

- padded
- pre-explained
- softened into nothing

Just:

> “That didn’t sit right with me.”

And then you stop.

You don’t rush to justify it.

You don’t explain it ten different ways.

You let it land as a clean signal.

---

If you tend to come in strong…

You don’t suppress yourself.

You reduce the weight.

You separate the moment from everything that came before it.

Not:

- “You always…”
- “This is what you’re like…”

Just:

> “In that moment, this is what it felt like for me.”

You take the pressure off the delivery  
so the point has a chance to be heard.

---

If you tend to over-explain…

You trust that less is actually clearer.

You don’t build a case.

You don’t try to control how it will be received.

You say it once, cleanly.

And you leave space.

That space is uncomfortable.

But it’s also where understanding has a chance to happen.

---

If you tend to misread…

You pause before meaning locks in.

You notice the feeling, but you don’t let it define the moment.

Instead of reacting to what it _feels like it means_, you check what is actually there.

> “Are you saying this… or am I adding to it?”

You give the moment a chance to be what it is  
before turning it into something else.

---

## 7. Holding When It Wobbles

None of this removes reaction.

That’s not the goal.

The goal is to stop reaction from _running the entire interaction_.

---

Now, something important happens when you start doing this.

At first, it won’t feel like it’s working.

The other person may still:

- react
- misunderstand
- shift things

And this is where most people fall back into the old pattern.

Because the pull is strong.

To:

- explain more
- push harder
- withdraw again

---

This is the exact point where the shift either holds…  
or collapses.

---

So you hold something simple.

You don’t chase.

You don’t collapse.

You don’t escalate to force it through.

You stay with your position.

Calm. Clear. Steady.

---

You might say:

> “I’m not trying to turn this into something bigger. I just want you to see this part.”

Or:

> “I’m not attacking you. I’m telling you what that was like for me.”

And then you stay there.

---

Not repeating it louder.

Not reshaping it.

Not abandoning it.

Just holding it.

---

This is where most people underestimate what’s happening.

Because it looks small.

But it changes the structure of the interaction.

---

You are no longer:

- adapting before the moment
- reacting inside the moment
- regretting after the moment

You are staying present _through_ it.

---
## 8. What This Becomes

And when you do that, something shifts in the system.

Not always immediately.

But reliably.

---

There is less distortion to react to.

Less pressure to defend against.

Less confusion about what is actually being said.

---

And even if the other person doesn’t change straight away…

You have already stepped out of the eggshell pattern.

---

You are no longer walking carefully to avoid something.

You are standing clearly in what is actually happening.

---

From there, something important becomes possible.

You can see the other person again.

Not as:

- “the one I have to manage”
- “the one I have to avoid triggering”

But as someone who is also reacting inside the same system.

---

They may still:

- get it wrong
- misread
- react badly

But now you are not pulled into reshaping yourself around that.

You stay anchored.

---

And this is where real freedom starts to come back.

Not the kind where nothing ever goes wrong.

The kind where:

> you can be yourself  
> and stay in the room at the same time

---

That’s the shift.

Not perfect communication.

Not perfect understanding.

Something more stable than that.

---

You become someone who can:

- say what matters
- hear what’s said
- stay grounded when it wobbles

---

And from there, the relationship has a chance to breathe again.

Not because the eggshells disappeared.

But because you stopped needing to walk on them.

---

**Be today, who you will respect tomorrow.**
